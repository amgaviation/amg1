# Design and Marketing

Use this guide for landing pages, marketing sites, product launches, and conversion-focused redesigns.

## Start with comprehension

A first-time visitor should understand within roughly five seconds:

- who the product or company serves;
- what valuable outcome it provides;
- why it is credible or different;
- what action to take next.

Make the primary message specific enough to exclude the wrong audience. Avoid vague prestige language, empty superlatives, and headlines that require the next three sections to explain them.

## Build the narrative

Use the smallest sequence that resolves the buyer’s questions:

1. Promise: outcome, audience, and primary action.
2. Context: the problem or cost of the current state.
3. Mechanism: how the offering creates the outcome.
4. Proof: verified evidence, demonstration, process, or product detail.
5. Scope: services, capabilities, plans, or use cases without repetition.
6. Objections: risk, fit, process, timing, or frequently asked questions.
7. Action: a clear next step with realistic expectations.

Change this order when the product requires a different decision path. Do not force every project into the same template.

## Create visual distinction

- Choose one strong visual premise tied to the brand or product.
- Use a clear grid with intentional exceptions, not random asymmetry.
- Use type for hierarchy and pacing, not merely decoration.
- Alternate density and quiet space to create rhythm.
- Let media demonstrate, orient, or create atmosphere; avoid irrelevant stock imagery.
- Reserve high-contrast color and motion for decisions and narrative peaks.
- Make premium work feel edited. Fewer, stronger elements usually outperform decorative excess.

## Use reference-site principles responsibly

Motion-led editorial sites often succeed through a coordinated system rather than one effect:

- a strong opening proposition;
- oversized but controlled typography;
- cinematic media crops and masks;
- scroll scenes that reveal information progressively;
- restrained navigation and calls to action;
- a small family of custom easings and repeated transition logic;
- section-to-section contrast that keeps a long page legible.

Adopt the principles that serve the brief. Create a new composition and motion story for the target brand.

## Avoid generic generated design

Reject these defaults unless the brief specifically calls for them:

- purple-blue gradients, glowing blobs, and indiscriminate glass panels;
- every section as a centered heading followed by three rounded cards;
- excessive pills, badges, icons, and floating decorations;
- giant headlines with weak meaning or unusable mobile wrapping;
- repeated claims with different headings;
- motion on every element, scroll hijacking, or novelty cursors that impair use;
- fake dashboards, invented metrics, fabricated reviews, and nonfunctional calls to action;
- landing-page aesthetics imposed on dense operational products.

## Copy and conversion details

- Use one primary action per decision point. Secondary actions must have a clear purpose.
- Make button labels describe the result, not just “Learn more.”
- Keep paragraphs short enough to scan and long enough to be credible.
- Put proof near the claim it supports.
- Explain form expectations, privacy implications, and next steps when relevant.
- Preserve truthful scope. A polished interface must not imply capabilities that do not exist.

## Marketing acceptance gate

- The hero communicates audience, value, and action without scrolling.
- Page sections each answer a distinct buyer question.
- No material claim is duplicated or unsupported.
- The primary conversion is obvious on desktop and mobile.
- Visual storytelling supports the offering rather than competing with it.
- Metadata, headings, crawlable text, and structured data match the page’s actual purpose.
